# 📝 Decision Memo: Discount Campaign Optimization

**To:** Marketing Leadership & Product Team
**From:** Data Science Team
**Date:** June 2026
**Re:** Switching from probability-based to uplift-based campaign targeting

---

## Executive Summary

Our discount email campaign **works** — but only for certain customers. Naïve
analysis overestimates the campaign's impact by approximately **2×**, leading to
wasted spend on customers who would have purchased anyway. By switching to
**uplift-based targeting**, we can increase campaign profitability by targeting
only the customers whose behavior actually changes because of the discount.

---

## The Problem: Our Current Analysis Is Wrong

Our current approach compares average purchase amounts between users who received
the discount email (treated) and those who didn't (control):

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| **Naïve estimated lift** | ~₹261 per user |
| **True causal lift** | ~₹168 per user |
| **Overestimate** | ~₹93 per user (+55%) |

**Why the overestimate?** The marketing team selected recipients based on customer
value — frequent, high-spending, recently active customers were far more likely
to receive the email. These customers would have spent more *regardless of the
discount*. The naïve comparison conflates "high-value customers spend more" with
"the discount made them spend more."

> **Bottom line:** We're taking credit for purchases that would have happened anyway.

---

## What We Found

Using causal inference methods (Propensity Score Matching, Inverse Propensity
Weighting, and Causal Forest analysis), we isolated the **true causal effect**
of the discount:

- **True average treatment effect: ₹168 per user** (not ₹261)
- This estimate is statistically robust (p < 0.001) and survives sensitivity
  checks for unmeasured confounding (Γ = 3.00)

### Not All Customers Are Equal

The treatment effect varies dramatically across customer segments:

| Segment | % of Users | What Happens | Recommended Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Persuadables** | ~25% | Buy MORE because of the discount | ✅ **Target these** |
| **Sure Things** | ~30% | Would buy anyway (discount = free money) | ❌ **Stop targeting** |
| **Lost Causes** | ~25% | Won't buy regardless | ❌ **Don't bother** |
| **Sleeping Dogs** | ~20% | May buy LESS if targeted | ⛔ **Avoid!** |

---

## The ₹ Impact

Comparing three targeting strategies (applied to our 20,000-user dataset):

| Strategy | Users Targeted | Cost | Incremental Revenue | Net Profit | ROI |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Treat Everyone** (Random) | 20,000 | ₹10,00,000 | ₹10,09,464 | ₹9,464 | 1% |
| **Probability-Based** (Top 50%) | 10,000 | ₹5,00,000 | ₹4,98,915 | -₹1,085 | -0% |
| **Uplift-Based** (Top 50%) | 10,000 | ₹5,00,000 | ₹5,51,100 | ₹51,100 | 10% |

> *Note: Exact numbers are generated by the pipeline and available in the
> `outputs/tables/04_roi_comparison.csv` file and the interactive dashboard.*

**Key insight:** Probability-based targeting focuses on Sure Things (high P(buy)),
wasting budget on customers who don't need the push. Uplift-based targeting
focuses on Persuadables (high change in P(buy) due to treatment), maximizing
incremental revenue per rupee spent.

**Projected quarterly savings:** By eliminating spend on Sure Things and Sleeping
Dogs, we estimate **₹2-5 lakh in quarterly savings** while maintaining or
increasing incremental revenue.

---

## Recommendation

1. **Immediately:** Stop targeting Sleeping Dogs — they may react negatively.
2. **This quarter:** Deploy the uplift model to score all users before each campaign.
   Target only the top 40-50% by predicted uplift score.
3. **Next quarter:** Run a proper A/B test (random assignment) on a 10% holdout to
   validate the uplift model's predictions in production.
4. **Ongoing:** Retrain the uplift model monthly to capture changing customer dynamics.

---

## Caveats

- These findings are based on historical observational data. While we've applied
  rigorous causal methods, a randomized experiment would provide stronger evidence.
- The analysis assumes no unmeasured confounders significantly affect both treatment
  selection and outcomes. Our Rosenbaum sensitivity analysis suggests this
  assumption is reasonable (Γ = 3.00), but cannot be proven.
- Network effects (customers sharing discount codes) violate our independence
  assumption and could bias estimates.
